1. Executive Summary

Spotter is a fitness aid company that makes products for gym goers. We are mainly marketing towards young and middle aged individuals that work out. Our product will allow these people to be able to review their form as well as keep a workout history.

1.2 Mission

We strive to reduce workout injuries while improving the gym experience. We want to give people with less experience and/or time to be able to keep track of their workouts and improve on it safely.

1.3 Product Summary

Our product, Spotter, utilizes models to check an individual’s form and point out their mistakes accordingly. The Spotter app also keeps track of an individual’s workout history and displays the information to the user, using this information the app could also create a future workout plan, which is tailored towards the individual’s goals. Actual implementation and detailed information will be in Section 4. Product Description.

1.4 Market Opportunity

Spotter will be sold to all types of gym owners: commercial, individual, specialty and boutique. Individual, specialty and boutique gyms will be targeted first as they are small scaled and easier to adapt to changes. Spotter will gain traction through fitness shows and customer feedback. As we have little to no competition, it will be relatively easy for us to market. Commercial gyms will be targeted last, due to their large scale and inflexibility to incorporate new equipment into their gyms. Details on the market and opportunities will be in Section 3 Market Overview and Section 7 Competitive Analysis.

1.5 Traction Summary

        Potential users were excited by the idea of having personalized workout plans regardless of their skill level. Spotter app was designed to be as simple to use as possible, in order to counter our biggest competitor: people doing nothing. Some gyms described potential interest if gym goers showed enough interest. Details on traction will be in Section 10 Traction.

2. Company Synopsis

2.1 Primary Customer

Spotter’s primary customers will be gym owners, regardless the type and scale of the gyms. The primary users of Spotter will be gym goers.

2.2 Primary Customer Persona

Our primary customer is going to be the gym owners, as they are the people who are largely going to be buying our product. Since Spotter can track improper form and prevent injuries, gym owners will see an overall decrease on workout injuries. The benefit they get from buying our product is that they are giving their members a better workout experience, and can therefore charge more for membership fees, or at least attract more potential members. However, the primary users of our product are the gym goers. These are the people who will rely on our product to track their workout performance.

2.3 Job-To-Be-Done

We want to give gym goers a convenient way to track and improve their workouts. This includes the ability to monitor form in order to prevent injury.

2.4 Key Element of Solution

A key element to improving workout performance is exercising with proper form. We aim to deliver users real time analysis of their form, in order to prevent injury and aid them in improving workout results. We also aim to provide an automatic way of recording workout statistics, so that users can easily access their workout history, and form new goals and workout regimens based on this information. Our overall objective is to give people a personalized workout experience.

2.5 Competitive Advantages

Our product is the only one on the market that is actually able to monitor a person’s form in real time, and automatically analyze where weak points in a person’s form lie, and where potential injury may occur. Our product also automates the task of recording a user’s workout, which not many products on the market are able to do. To see how Spotter compares with other brands, please go to Section 7 Competitive Analysis.

3. Market Overview

        There are over 57 million individuals in the US who go to the gym, and this number has been rising steadily from 32.8 million in 2000. The average growth rate is around 1.5 million new individuals per year going to the gym. In the US, there are over 36,000 gyms that are membership based, and are either stand alone organizations or chain based. These gyms earned over 27.6 billion dollars in 2015 and this number continues to increase. Globally gyms raked in more than 75 billion dollars in 2012.

3.1 Key Trends

        With the rising number of new gym memberships and such a huge market in gyms, the gyms will aggressively try to market to obtain new members. Having free analytics and form correction is a very attractive feature, which gyms will gravitate to in order to get new members.

There is a new trend of incorporating technology into the gym, which is shown through the usage of pedometers on phones/smartwatches and FitBits. More people want to  be able to know how well and how much they are doing inside a gym. Spotter sees this trend and incorporates this within our app by showing in depth history of one’s workout.

Boutique and private gyms are also on the rise as more people are willing to pay a bit more to receive better and more personalized service than regular commercial gyms. In 2015, boutique gyms made up 21% of all gyms. Personal trainers will see a growth of 24% before 2020, which is caused by the demand of more personalized health services and increased number of gym goers.

3.2 Competitors Success

Fitbit: their success is able to build a community revolving around fitness and made it into an social experience. Their specialization in fitness wearables allowed them to evolve and upgrade faster than generic smart watches, such as Apple Watch and Samsung Gear series.

4. How the Product Works

        Our product mainly works in two ways. First of all, we have an iOS application which initiates the workouts and manages users’ workout progress. In the phone only mode, users will be able to use the cameras on their phones to record their workout. Then the phone sends these data to our backend database which will do machine learning(Open Pose) to analyze if users’ forms are good or bad. Based on these results the app will display a set of instructions for the user to correct their form. Then, the app will log these results and progress in the forms of charts on users’ phones so that they can track their own progress. In external camera mode, we first set up these cameras on the racks in the perfect spot. Users will still need their phone application to use the whole setup. However, they won’t use the cameras on their phones to video record them. Instead, the pre-setup external camera will record. Similarly, after the workout, the app will log all the progress and results.

        The main difference between these two modes is that in the external camera mode, the camera can be set up in a way that it’s at the perfect position and angle. This will help with our machine learning program. Also, in the future, we can also upgrade the external camera for better quality.

5. Revenue Model

        Our main revenue model is subscription. We charge gyms twice what the analytics will cost us to run. We assess gyms by looking at the number of squat racks they own and the hours they are open,  Individuals who have gyms at their homes can pay $12.99/month for the service.  With commercial gyms, Spotter is looking at 100% margins. For individual or home gyms, the margins is still good, unless someone is working out over 9 hours a day everyday at home without rests, Spotter’s services is profitable.

Our secondary revenue model is the physical devices, which are $100 a piece and $50 for each additional camera.  Spotter also includes add-on services. Currently we only have one, which is being able to upload video to our servers without using the Spotter device. This video upload service is $2.99/month.

We will charge gyms twice our costs to run analytics, which we calculate this through: $/hour *  # of squat racks * (70% of # of hours open/wk) * 4wks/month * 1.1(10% overhead). For example, a gym with 15 squat racks, open 20 hours a day will cost around $323 to run the servers and the gym will be charged $646.

The average gym has around 15 squat racks, with membership typically around $45 and between 3000 to 5000 members, so Spotter’s cost to them is around 22 cents or .48% of their overall revenue, so it is very cheap for gyms.

        

                For the device itself, with the price breakdown on the left, Spotter is looking at around 90% margins and costing us around $52 per piece. With wholesale prices in the future, it will be clear that the margins will be even bigger with lower cost to us. This way we can afford more promotional sales to grab customers attention.

6. Operating Model

For our backend machine learning algorithm, we need some really powerful computers in order to give smooth real time analysis and feedback to the users. We have several options for this, including Google Cloud Computing, Amazon EC2 etc. These services are mostly charged by the hours we use. We will cover this cost by collecting monthly subscription from our users. Depending on the frequency and time people use our services, we might do subtle adjustment to our monthly subscription price.

Besides this, our service is pretty independent. We don’t really need to maintain anything else beside proper iOS application update and some minor tweaks to our backend machine learning algorithm.

8. Customer Personas

8.1 Amateur Bodybuilder

This is the person who goes to the gym often, and really cares about improving their ability to lift weight. This person may already use their phone to record their workouts and plans their workouts to optimize performance gains and minimize the risk of injury.

8.2 Beginner at the Gym

This is the person who just started going to the gym, and is fairly uncomfortable with working out. They therefore might not know what weight they should begin lifting with, and because they have just started lifting, they may not have the greatest form. They may seek advice from personnel at the gym on how to work out, and would therefore be interested in having an app that also helps them with this process.

8.3 Regular Gymer

This is the person who goes to the gym often, and may not care very much about improving in lifting at all. However, they do care a lot about their overall fitness, and like that they can easily record their workouts and see their workout history. Having the validation that they are working out properly, is also important for them.

8.4 Coaches

Whether a high school sports team coach, or a personal trainer, this is the person who wants the ability to measure their athletes performance in a very simple way. Being able to see their athletes actually working out from anywhere gives them a very convenient way to be able to advise their athletes, in order to improve their performance.

9. Customer Acquisition

To find customers, we’re going to immediately offer our product to be used for mobile, and we’ll begin targeting smaller gyms for acquisition. Once we begin to get smaller gyms to begin purchasing the product (many of these will likely be specialty and boutique gyms) we will begin targeting the larger gyms. In addition, we’re hoping to form partnerships with schools and other nonprofits or teams, who would potentially like to use this product to monitor their athletes. Finally, this product could also be pitched to personal trainers, who could also use it to monitor their clients’ workouts.

In order to specifically target the gyms, we’d plan on physically going to many of the smaller gyms within the county to begin pitching the product, as well as just reaching out via email or phone to many other smaller gyms throughout the country. Once we got smaller gyms to begin purchasing the product, we’d start at least forming relationships with the larger gyms, to at least warm them up to the idea of purchasing our product, and hopefully getting them to commit to it. We’d also plan on attending many of the large fitness expos in order to display our product to people who own or work at gyms. Likewise, to grow the mobile version of our product right away, we were thinking of targeting several popular fitness influencers on Instagram for solicitation of our product. We could also use ads on most of the social media platforms to reach a large audience and display our product. If our product is received well, we could use this as extra evidence for gyms to buy into our product. In addition, if personal trainers and coaches see that our product is well-received, they are also more likely to begin using it with clients.

Lifetime Value of Each Customer (this is for gyms): $300/month * 12months/year * 20 years = $72,000

Customer Acquisition Costs: We don’t have a definite number on this yet, as we’d be selling our product to very different audiences, and would be targeting these audiences using different methods.

10. Traction

For our initial phase of product development, we focused on making sure that we could easily count reps, sets, weights and other metrics, as well as capture form monitoring, as when talking to customers, we learned that while form monitoring is a cool and unique feature, what we’re really trying to offer customers is a comprehensive workout experience. If people are going to capture data on their workouts at the gym, the basics need to be included as well. Many of the people we talked to already record stats on their workouts, however they have to do so manually. Customers we talked to were really excited by the idea of having an automated way to track their workout history. Along with this, people were also excited by the idea of having workout plans personalized for them based on their workout history, which is a feature we would quickly add in a future iteration of this product. Likewise, doing nothing, or not using our product, is probably the biggest competitor we face, and to counter that, we tried to make our app as easy to use as possible. Users just have to scan a QR code to begin using the product. We hope that’s on par with other popular fitness trackers such as the fitbit and Apple Watch, which capture a lot of data without the user often realizing it. To go along with this, we found that even when we talked to customers who rarely work out, and who never record their workout statistics, we were surprised to find that many of them would still be interested in trying our product at the gym at least once, largely out of a curiosity of seeing if they really are working out properly. We think that if we can make the product very simple to use, we could get a lot of these customers to continue to use the product. Finally, the idea of being able to use this device anywhere and for any sport seemed to be the idea that most excited the customers we talked to, which is why we developed a mobile only version of this product, and why we’re considering expanding into other sectors outside the gym upon further development of this product.
